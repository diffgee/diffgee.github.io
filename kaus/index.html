Designing a journey which serves the users needs

Meeting key user needs through an end-to-end process

Context

This is an educational design project. Kaus (a fictional insurance company) only sold their insurance packages through regional agents. They’re losing sales by not having an e-commerce website for selling directly to consumers.

Outcome

Leveraging the Design Thinking framework, I identified key product features to best align business and customer goals. Through a process of testing and iteration, I designed a journey for users to confidently navigate complex financial choices.

I'm pleased to share my design process below, but please note, the page is in development

Process

Empathize

Using the Design Thinking framework, my initial goal was to empathize with the internal team and the users. Employing secondary research, competitive analysis and user interviews, I gained insights needed to define the problem, as well as identifying and validating assumptions.

Secondary Research and Competitive analysis

I conducted a literature review of existing research within the insurance industry. Several key insights were gained at this stage regarding consumer demographics, motivations, and buying behavior.

Key Insights
 • Most insurance is not purchased voluntarily
• There is a preference to shop online and purchase in person/by phone

I also conducted a competitive analysis of the markets approach to e-commerce solutions. This provided insights into competitors solutions, as well as a baseline which likely informs consumers expectations when shopping for and purchasing insurance online.

Key Insights
 • Strong emphasis on quote process
• Natural language policies
• “Same team” assurances (“You’re in safe hands”, “Become part of a family”, etc)

Provisional personas and interviews

Equipped with the knowledge gained from my research up to this point, I began the process of defining and empathizing with the user. I wanted to ensure my inquiries would be relevant and impactful, so I started by creating two provisional personas which reflected my understanding of users based on the data collected so far.

With these archetypes in mind, I defined my primary user research goals, questions, and methodologies. I also identified my assumptions regarding users.

High level user interview goal
Identify shared user needs in order to define the specific problem to be solved.

Using Google Forms, I devised a survey and obtained a small amount of quantitative data which aligned with the demographic descriptions found in secondary research.

For the user interviews, I devised a series of open-ended, and closed-ended questions driven by my research up to this point in order to obtain qualitative data. I would also improvise further questioning based on user responses during the interview.

I recruited and conducted this interview with three participants. While this was a small sample, I was pleasantly surprised by the quality of information gained, which opened paths my previous research and assumptions overlooked.

Synthisizing the findings

I synthesized my interview findings by creating empathy maps. This assisted in finding overlap between interviewees.

In order to share my findings I created a summary of the consensus identified in the user interviews.

User Personas

Using the information I gained from this user research, I created a user persona which would serve to promote user centered design decisions.

Finally, I created a slide deck summarizing my findings and deliverables up to this stage. This was an important asset in communicating and documenting this process in a centralized way.

My UX research efforts to this stage gave me a firm understanding of the business needs, market, and most importantly, a strong understanding of user needs, goals and pains. It would serve as a firm ground from which I could then define the problem.

Define

While the brief provided a business-centered problem statement, as a designer I needed to develop a human-centered problem statement. Aligning these will provide a well-defined product. Here’s how I did that.

I first created a storyboard to develop a use case scenario from the perspective of my user persona.

With this and the user persona in hand, I began identifying requirements of the product from the users perspective.

I categorized the identified user requirements as “data”, “function”, and “qualities”. I also identified project constraints at this time.

I then developed a series of POV statements, which helps turn my user insights into human-centered actionable problems.

I then further broke the POV statements down into a series of How Might We statements. These would further refine my design problem, and leave me well-prepared for impactful ideation.

Ideation

With a set of clear, focused, user-centered design problems to solve I began brainstorming ideas.

After generating a workable number of solutions, I began culling and taking my constraints into mind in order to narrow in on an appropriate design solution for the project.

Constraints
• Can I build it within my timeframe?
• Can I build it as a solo designer?
• Can it be implemented as a responsive website?

With all this in mind, I determined an appropriate product would be a homepage as well as an auto insurance quote process. Based on all the research up to this stage and the ideation process, I believed this would be the most impactful and achievable section of the product in which to focus on a building a great user experience.

Design

In order to begin building a prototype, I started by listing out my UI requirements.

This document defined:
• What pages to build
• High level requirements
• Detailed requirements

Information Architecture

I then needed to establish my Information Architecture. I wanted to ensure the categories and labels I used in the UI would make sense to users. I conducted a card sort test in order to validate this part of the design.
I wanted to make sure I understood how the pages I would be building fit into the larger picture. I built a user flow, task flow, and site map to see how the pages I was building fit into the product as a whole.

With my UI requirements and a good understanding of how the screens fit into the larger design, I began developing wireframes.

I started by sketching, which let me rapidly try out variations and explore different ideas.

Design System and Wireframes

At this stage it was clear to me that forms would be an important design pattern. The quote process would naturally rely on user input, and forms are a common solution to allowing users to submit information. I conducted a literature review of common design patterns in building a form. This ensured I would be using a best practices approach while designing this critical part of the product. Insights here informed the use of a progress tracker, clear primary actions and progressive disclosure.

From here I chose my strongest ideas from sketching and developed mid-fidelity wireframes.

While not a focus of this case study, I developed the visual style, branding, and a logo at this time. I emphasized user desired product traits uncovered during the research phase, primarily trustworthiness and reliability. I designed a UI kit to ensure consistency and communicate design decisions.

All of the appropriate assets were now in place to develop high fidelity screens, and finally a clickable prototype using Sketch.

My next step would be to test and validate the design with users.

Prototype

I also then created a prototype in Figma to begin user testing.

Validate

User Testing

I first created a User Test Plan. This document defined the testing objective, method, and user testing script.

After recruiting and running the test with three participants, I created an affinity map. This would help me identify an overall picture of the outcome of testing.

Results

Overall, the success rate and feedback indicated appropriate design decisions had been made up to this point.

I did identify some related comments from users regarding the progress bar in the quote process. One user wanted more functionality from it, and another commented that it could be more visually communicative.

Because the progress bar component would be used across all insurance quote processes, I felt refining and optimizing this would be a good use of resources.

 

Iterate

Results from user testing indicated the progress bar in the quote process was a helpful feature, but had potential to provide even greater value to the users. My goal then was to enhance the communicative function of the progress bar.

Incorporating previous iterations of the progress bar with some newly ideated solutions, I added further visual communication on the steps completed, as well as the current step.

Unlocking Insights

This project shows the power of the Design Thinking framework. Each step brought insights and understanding which enabled the development of a product that didn’t just meet business needs, but resulted in an engaging user-friendly product.

This case study highlights a few key takeaways that stood out to me as a designer.

 

Minimal resources can provide meaningful returns.
As a researcher I’ve been involved in massive projects, 1,000’s of participant interviews conducted over a period of years and organized into enormous quantitative datasets. And yet, here in this project qualitative interviews of a few subjects proved extremely valuable. Bigger isn’t always better. Use your resources (and time) wisely for greatest impact.

 

Don’t reinvent the wheel
Common problems in design emerge again and again. In this project I needed to build an engaging and intuitive form for users to input their information. I am hardly the first designer to approach this problem. Making use of existing design patterns speeds up development, and provides users a familiarity which improves their experience.

 

Stay open minded
It’s easy to have immediate solutions come to mind when designing a product, but it’s important to not get overly attached. This is one of the strengths of the Design Thinking framework. The emphasis on first understanding, empathizing, and defining lays the groundwork for generating the most impactful solutions.
