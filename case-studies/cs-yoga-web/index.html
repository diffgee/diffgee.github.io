Designing a responsive website and branding for a small business

Leveraging the Design Thinking framework to unify business and user needs

Overview

Purpose: Client project

Objective: Design a responsive website and branding for a local small business

Approach: Design Thinking framework

Role: Designer- responsible for entire design process and all deliverables

Introduction

A blank slate is an exciting challenge

I was approached by a local yoga instructor to design a website and build branding for their business. This was an exciting opportunity, as at this time they had not established a web presence or branding, which allowed beginning the project with a fresh slate. I applied the Design Thinking framework to my approach, which began with a research phase.

Research

Taking a look around is the first step

Client Meeting
To start the project, I wanted to establish a thorough understanding of the clients goals. I approached our initial meeting much like a user interview, aiming to empathize with the clients needs for the product. This led to valuable insights about the problems the client was hoping to solve:

their online presence was controlled by outside businesses she contracted with

they were reliant on verbal communication to inform potential customers of their own class offerings

there was no single source for informing people of her various class offerings

In addition to identifying these pain points, I also gained further understanding of:

current customer journey

project resources and budget

deliverable expectations

brand voice

The end result was aligning the our project goals and expectations.

Secondary Research
I then began conducting secondary research of similar business websites. I believe this is a very valuable step in the design process. It allows for further empathizing with both the client and the customers by establishing the experience a current user is likely to have, and therefore expect. I identified several common themes including:

Class information provided
Most sites provide thorough class explanations, as yoga uses terms a layman would be unfamiliar with

Robust about sections
Yoga instruction sites often provide an emphasis on the individual instructor

Branding tone
Most sites emphasize health and wellness (mental and physical), and have a visual style which promotes calmness

I presented my findings to the client, which guided us to consensus for the next steps of the project. The primary deliverables decided on were providing a logo, and high-fidelity website screens for desktop and mobile.

Branding

Capturing a voice that speaks to the user

The client was eager to establish a brand identity for the business, with a logo being the main deliverable. My primary goals for the end result were focused on the users experience.

  • Is it legible?
  • Does it readily communicate the service offered?
  • Is it versatile? (Eg. variety of sizes, variations, print/digital uses)
  • Does it communicate the clients desired tone?

After sketching and designing iterations in vector design software, I established three logos to present the client. The client was very pleased with one of the designs, so my next step was to validate the design with user interviews.

I wrote a short user interview script in order to validate that the usability design goals were met for the logo. I conducted this interview with 8 individuals. Additionally, I presented the logo to a group critique of design peers.

Several insights were gained at this stage.

  • The logo was readily identified as offering yoga classes
  • The font was legible and users were able to accurately identify the business name
  • The desired tone was identified by most users (personable), however, a number of users also identified it as having a juvenile tone due to the use of the script font

Many iterations were explored during this phase.

This led to a final iteration of the logo, replacing the script font with a serif font. This ensured appropriate legibility without ambiguous letters (ie I vs L), and also conveyed a more sophisticated tone now desired by the client.

Website Design

A primary connector between business and user

With the logo deliverable in place, the next phase consisted on designing the website. The primary goal established for the site was for the client to have a single destination they could share to provide their class schedule. I started by using the information from research and client meetings to develop a site map.

The sitemap was helpful for several reasons at this stage

  • It established the number of expected screens to design
  • It established the necessary content, allowing the client to begin writing (eg class descriptions, about section, policies, etc)


I also developed a User Story Map in order to further define the websites functionality and content.

This was a valuable resource for my design, as it allowed me to stay focused on the primary user goals established: learn about and sign up for the clients yoga classes.

With these resources in place, I began designing the identified screens in Figma.


I started by designing the landing page. Because this part of the site would be the likely entry point for the user, I wanted to make sure to clearly communicate

  • This is a site for yoga classes
  • A clear CTA (call to action) for directing the user to detailed class information
  • A welcoming, on brand tone

If the user were to continue scrolling, I wanted to continue providing varied reasons to direct them to the class information page (a primary goal of the site).

This included sections which:

  • communicate primary benefits of taking part in a yoga class
  • testimonials in order to provide social proof of the service
  • credentials and an about the instructor to provide credibility. This included designing badges to visually communicate the clients relevant education


The other page in which Ill focus on in this case study is the class information page. This page was to serve as the primary site destination. I identified the needed information by referencing my secondary research, client meetings, and several short user interviews focusing on what information they would want to know prior to signing up for a yoga class.

The required information boiled down to

  • Class name
  • Class location
  • Class schedule (day/time)
  • Class description
  • Cost
  • Class difficulty
  • How to sign up

One weakness I had identified in the research phase was that sites often presented this information as a large chunk of text. I wanted to make learning about class information a more intuitive process for users. I chose to design cards to display this information, with accompanying information icons. This design choice allowed me to present the information in a flexible, repeated layout while also being able to emphasize informational hierarchy.

I also included a section providing a broad overview of the class schedule, in order to give a user the ease of seeing the bare essentials (ie where and when).

Following the design of the desktop screens, I adapted the screens for mobile viewing. The informational hierarchy proved versatile on this transition due to the use of cards.

Client handoff
At this stage I reviewed the high-fidelity screens with the client. They were very pleased with the results, and felt it would be a valuable resource for potential and current customers.

Conclusion

Another project, another learning opportunity

In the process of designing this website and branding, Id like to highlight a few key takeaways.

Stay vigilant in keeping the focus on users
Stakeholder input and buy-in is critical to any projects success. As a user advocate though, designers need to stay vigilant to keep the users experience a primary focus. This can be a particular challenge when developing branding, where individual aesthetic preferences can compete with legibility and communicative design.

Work within the limits of your resources
Certain product design ideas were not able to be pursued in the project due to limited resources. While further user touch points like automated newsletters, and in-site class sign up and payment carry value (both to the client and users), they werent able to be included in the design due to development and resource constraints.


Thanks again for your interest. I hope you got to know me a little better as a designer and how I make use of design thinking.
