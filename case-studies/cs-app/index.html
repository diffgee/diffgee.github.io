End to end mobile app design

Building a lifestyle app catering to food lovers

Overview

Purpose: Student project

Objective: End to end design for a lifestyle app

Approach: Design Thinking framework

Role: Designer- responsible for entire design process and all deliverables

Introduction

Product design from start to finish. The whole enchilada.

In this project I took on the challenge of an end-to-end mobile app design for a fictional user who enjoys looking at photos of food. I approached the process using the Design Thinking framework.

Research

Understanding the product by understanding the users

Secondary research
As with many projects, I decided to start with some research. I began by completing secondary research and literature reviews regarding user behavior and psychology in the context of posting and viewing food on social media. Some rather interesting findings were discovered at this stage. Because my goal was fairly open ended at this stage, I decided to condense my fact finding into a board allowing me to sort the findings into categories.

Reviewing both quantitative and qualitative data, I wanted to base the initial product concept on findings indicating a user need. There were a few particular findings which stood out to me:

  • Nearly 25% of all instagram users photograph their food for a food diary, making it the most photographed subject on instagram
  • A broader finding supported that data, indicating 1 in 4 consumers regularly photograph their food for a diary
  • 86% of millennials will try a new restaurant after seeing food-related content online
  • Users currently put a premium on product simplicity in the food lifestyle market

At this stage I felt this data supported pursuing an app which would provide a streamlined food journaling experience, with the additional feature of helping users find a desirable restaurant.

I also wanted to apply these findings to a user persona in order to being into focus the expected user of such a product.

Define

Product scope in focus

I then set out to better define the product scope. I started this by creating a series of How Might We statements, in order to identify features which would delight users and possible solutions to bring those features to life.

This process ended up landing me quite a few possible features. I culled these possibilities down at this stage. I kept a few considerations in mind to help me do this. I continued to have the persona and research findings guide the needed features, namely the goal of a streamlined food journal and a feature to assist in eating out. Further, my goal was to build out a minimum viable product (MVP), and so features unnecessary to the core functioning of the product were set aside.

The refined product feature set from this process was

  • Create a streamlined journal entry process
  • Spotlight photo content
  • Integrate into Google Maps for finding nearby restaurants

Design

Bringing the product to life

I started by creating a app navigation map (ie. Sitemap). This would act as a very low-fi design, and help identify each screen Iâ€™d need to design.

I then began designing the screens in Figma. I kept to the Google Material design system for this project. I also kept the design patterns in line with other similar lifestyle apps in order to keep the UI intuitive for users.

While designing the screens, I identified UI components which would have likely reuse across screens and used appropriate Figma features to streamline the design process. This included components, variants, properties, styles and auto-layout.

The end result was a series of screens allowing the user to:

  • Log in/create account
  • Navigate journals and entries with photos prominently placed
  • Create and edit journal entries
  • Find nearby food inspired by their food entry

I also then created a prototype in Figma to more fully communicate the app behavior.

Conclusion

Reflecting on the lessons learned

A couple lessons stood out to me after designing this end to end application.

Start by focusing on a minimum viable product (MVP)
Doing this lets a designer maximize their resources in making sure the fundamental product idea is sound. Additional wanted features can be identified in the initial design process, but setting them aside for future inclusion ensures the core product is given its best chance of success.

Make use of existing design systems and flexible UI tooling
By using the design system resources provided by Google I was able to quickly get app screens built in a way which was grounded in solid proven design fundamentals. This included components like the top navigation bar, bottom app bar, and the use of cards for informative UI navigation. Additionally, making use of modern design tools in Figma allowed for creating a flexible UI toolbox.
