Adding a new feature to YouTube Music

Seamless feature addition using the Design Thinking framework

Overview

Purpose: Student project

Objective: Design a feature to add value to an existing product

Approach: Design Thinking framework

Role: Designer- responsible for entire design process and all deliverables

Introduction

A blank slate is an exciting challenge

It is often the case that a designer is asked to work within an existing product and design system. In this project, I identify a value adding feature to YouTube Music and implement it in a way which is cohesive with the established design system. In this case study I’ll take you through my process of adding a social music listening feature to YouTube Music.

Empathize

Understanding user behaviors and desires

Behavioral Research
In order to begin defining what feature I would design for YouTube Music, I started by researching how people currently use music streaming products. I’ve found secondary research to be a critical step in starting down the correct design path.

One of the key findings of this research is that people frequently engage in listening to music in a social setting.

I honed in on this finding, and began researching how current products address this common user behavior.

During my product comparison research I created a document identifying the products and their key features.

Communicating Findings
I wanted to have a resource which brought together all these research findings in a sharable way for a diverse audience, so I created a slide deck summarizing the background of the product YouTube Music, the user behavior insights, and product comparisons for social music listening features.

Using these findings I identified one product in particular, Spotify Blend, as being highly relevant to this new potential feature to YouTube Music.

Briefly, Spotify Blend is a feature which allows two users to share a playlist. This playlist is created by an algorithm which looks at each users listening habits and attempts to “blend” their interests together into a single music source.

I wanted to learn more about the user experience of this feature.

  • Did people enjoy it? Why?
  • Is there something that detracts from their experience when using Blend?
  • Are there requests for expanded function?

 

Lucky for me, Spotify maintains a forum dedicated to users providing feedback on product features.

I began collecting all relevant user comments, and organized it all into an affinity map. Here, I recorded the user quotes and identified 6 broad categories to organize the comments. This became a valuable resource in identifying users pains and gains (ie. what they liked, and what they didn’t).

At this stage, the direction of the product feature was taking shape but still lacked proper definition. In order to further define the feature, I wanted to gain deeper insight into the needs of a user. I relied on three main deliverables to achieve this:

  • 4W’s and a POV statement (who, what, where, why)
  • User persona
  • User story scenario

Now that I had a deeper understanding of the user, I was able to define a specific problem users face.

People enjoy listening to music in groups, but struggle to easily find common ground.

Define

A defined feature sets the stage for design

I began brainstorming further subsets of problems people face with social music listening, and coming up with possible solutions which could be incorporated as a product feature. I used a series of How Might We statements to achieve this. I then identified a narrow set of promising solutions to begin incorporating into a defined product feature.

I then created a user scenario. I wanted to further define the feature through a user centered view. I wanted to communicate the user problem, user need, and solution provided by the feature in a succinct manner.

In order to define the feature and how it worked, I created a user flow. This started as a flowchart, and in a later iteration product screens were incorporated to better communicate the process. I named the feature GroupRadio to communicate the social nature of the feature, and radio-like setup (ie. low effort , auto-curated music start).

Design

A primary connector between client and user

My main goals while designing the screens were:

  • Disruption to the existing product should be minimal. The feature addition should not be a lengthy overhaul of a product people are already familiar with.
  • The features behavior and look needs to fit existing design system.
    Users of the existing product should feel at home with the behavior of interactions and symbols.

  • The steps for use need to be minimal.
    A key finding during the research phase was the prominence of “low effort” music listeners. The perfect radio would have one button while providing a delightful user experience.


Where possible, I made use of existing resources and screens. When new assets and interactions were designed, I conformed them to mesh with the existing product.

Lastly, I used Figma to create a prototype which would allow me to user test the feature.

Validate

Reflecting on the design through user testing

In order to user test and validate the design, I recruited three individuals. Two were current users of YouTube Music, while one was unfamiliar with it. Individual testing sessions were conducted in person. I described the feature, a brief scenario, and asked the user to start a new GroupRadio. Upon completion of that task, I asked them to go through the process of adding another user.

The results of this testing session were mixed. Most users struggled to find the way to start the GroupRadio. However, the second task of adding a user was completed by all users without further difficulty. Additionally, even upon successful task completion users displayed confusion on the details of how the feature functioned, and how it differed from existing features such as a shared playlist.

Iterate

Leveraging findings to improve the user experience

The confusion regarding the nature of the feature is understandable. The differences between features like auto-generated playlists, streaming radio, and shared playlists are subtle but meaningful. Often, user interfaces might make use of tooltips to inform users of functionality. It hasn’t been a traditional approach of the YouTube Music app, nor did I feel it would give sufficient context to the user on fully understanding how this feature is distinct from others.

 

Youtube Music often highlights new features with emailed newsletters and landing pages. I decided to design a product feature landing page which could be used in this manner. This approach allowed me to succinctly display the product feature, it’s likely use cases, and how to use it- all in a way that was on-brand and undisruptive of the existing app experience.

Conclusion

Lessons learned and experience gained

In this case study I designed a feature addition, GroupRadio, for an existing product using the Design Thinking framework. Upon reflection, there are some key takeaways I’d highlight

Existing products, and their users, are a valuable resource
In developing a new feature, identifying the current product landscape is essential. There’s a lot to be learned by those who have designed similar product features. Further, the users of a similar product can be a goldmine in which to empathize with users delights and frictions.

The users experience is not confined to the app
In confronting users confusion about how this feature was distinct from other seemingly similar features, designing informational material outside the app proved beneficial. Using the medium of a website, rather than a small phone screen, gave the space to clearly display the feature use case, problems it solves, as well as how to use it. This allowed users a proper introduction to a novel feature.


Thanks again for your interest. I hope you got to know me a little better as a designer and how I make use of design thinking.
