End to end mobile app design

Building a lifestyle app catering to food lovers

Context

Millions of users engage with the food lifestyle market each day. In this project I identified a cohesive set of user needs in this domain, and designed an app to meet those needs with a delightful user experience.

Outcome

Leveraging the design thinking framework resulted in an end-to-end app design which addressed common user needs and desires. The end product, Plattr, allows users to save their food experiences to a journal in a streamlined fashion.

Process

Research

Goals

I wanted to understand the users of food related content online in order to define the project scope and goals.

Objectives

  • Determine the primary users
  • Learn what goals the users are aiming to accomplish
  • Identify what barriers users face in their journey

Methods

  • Secondary research
  • Competitive analysis
  • User interviews
See the FigJam file

Findings

Research synthesis

  • Nearly 25% of all instagram users photograph their food for a food diary, making it the most photographed subject on instagram

  • A broader finding supported that data, indicating 1 in 4 consumers regularly photograph their food for a diary

  • 86% of millennials will try a new restaurant after seeing food-related content online

  • Users currently put a premium on product simplicity in the food lifestyle market

Research conclusions

At this stage I felt this data supported pursuing an app which would provide a streamlined food journaling experience.

Define

In order to identify features which would delight users, I created a series of How Might We statements, and possible solutions to bring those features to life.

Culling from the generated ideas, a minimum viable product (MVP) feature set was determined.

Product features

  • Streamlined journal entry process
  • Spotlight photo content
  • Integrate into Google Maps

Design

I started by creating a sitemap. This would act as a very low-fi design, and help identify each screen needed.

Using Figma and the Google Material design system, I designed the screens needed for the specified user journey. I also kept the design patterns in line with other similar lifestyle apps in order to keep the UI intuitive for users.

While designing the screens, I identified UI components which would have likely reuse across screens and used appropriate Figma features to streamline the design process. This included components, variants, properties, styles and auto-layout.

The end result was a series of screens allowing the user to:

  • Log in/create account
  • Navigate journals and entries with photos prominently placed
  • Create and edit journal entries
  • Find nearby food inspired by their food entry

Prototype

I also then created a prototype in Figma to more fully communicate the app behavior.

Unlocking Insights

A couple lessons stood out to me after designing this end to end application.

Start by focusing on a minimum viable product (MVP)
Doing this lets a designer maximize their resources in making sure the fundamental product idea is sound. Additional wanted features can be identified in the initial design process, but setting them aside for future inclusion ensures the core product is given its best chance of success.

Make use of existing design systems and flexible UI tooling
By using the design system resources provided by Google I was able to quickly get app screens built in a way which was grounded in solid proven design fundamentals. This included components like the top navigation bar, bottom app bar, and the use of cards for informative UI navigation. Additionally, making use of modern design tools in Figma allowed for creating a flexible UI toolbox.
